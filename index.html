WIRE #441-B — CANTONMENT BUREAU, FRIDAY 17 OCT. 1941

Henderson's Railway Files Go Missing

BY OUR RAILWAY CORRESPONDENT — FILED AT 04:15 HRS

The study of the late Major Reginald Henderson at 'The Silver Oaks', Richards Town, was found in a state considerably more disordered than the Cantonment Police initially reported. Our correspondent has learned, through a source within the Railway Security Division who insists on anonymity, that a leather satchel bearing the insignia of the Kolar Gold Fields Audit Committee was absent from the premises when investigators arrived at dawn.

Major Henderson, whose duties placed him at the intersection of military logistics and the Mysore State Railway's wartime freight operations, had been conducting a quiet inquiry into discrepancies in the gold ore consignment records since August. The audit, commissioned directly by the Dewan's office, was considered highly sensitive — the Kolar mines supply a significant portion of the Crown's wartime reserves.

"The satchel contained three months of ledger transcripts, personal annotations and, we are told, a sealed letter addressed to the Resident Commissioner in Mysore. It was not found. It is not a small thing to lose," our source noted, declining further comment.

The Railway Security Division has declined to confirm or deny the existence of the audit. A spokesman offered only that Major Henderson's files were "a matter of ongoing administrative review." The Cantonment Police have not announced whether they consider the missing documents relevant to the death inquiry, now formally classified as suspicious.

Inspector Rao of the Cantonment Police has requested that any person with knowledge of the satchel's whereabouts present themselves voluntarily at the Cubbon Road police station before the evening of Saturday the 18th.

— END OF WIRE —

INTERCEPTED CORRESPONDENCE #92 — RICHARDS TOWN, 17 OCT. 1941

Silk Merchant Seen Near Silver Oaks

BY OUR RICHARDS TOWN CORRESPONDENT

Not one but three independent witnesses have placed Mr. Ratan Narayan, proprietor of the Chickpet Silk Emporium, in the vicinity of 'The Silver Oaks' on the night of Thursday the 16th of October. Mrs. D'Souza of No. 14 Richards Town Road reports seeing a motorcar matching the description of Mr. Narayan's black Standard Eight parked outside the bungalow gates at approximately a quarter past eleven. A rickshaw puller who works the Ulsoor junction corroborates this account, placing the vehicle there until nearly midnight.

Mr. Narayan, when confronted by this correspondent at his shop this morning, denied any visit to the Major's residence. "I was collecting a debt, yes — but through a messenger, not in person. I do not make house calls after dark," he stated, adjusting his spectacles with some agitation. He declined to name the messenger.

"The Major owed for three bolts of gold-thread silk — the finest in my inventory. I am a businessman. I do not kill my best debtors. That would be supremely counterproductive."

Records obtained from the Silk Emporium's receipt book — which Mr. Narayan allowed this correspondent to inspect briefly before snatching it back — show a line of credit extended to one 'R. Henderson, Esq.' dating to March of this year. The outstanding sum is not trifling: a figure approaching three hundred rupees, with accumulated interest, is indicated by the final entry, dated the 14th of October — two days before the Major's death.

Mr. Narayan's Standard Eight motorcar was observed by this correspondent outside the Cantonment Police station at nine o'clock this morning. He had not emerged by the time of going to press.

— END OF DISPATCH —

CONFIDENTIAL MEMO #7 — CANTONMENT SOCIAL DESK, 17 OCT. 1941

Memsahib's Alibi Disputed

BY OUR CANTONMENT SOCIAL CORRESPONDENT

Mrs. Eleanor Pemberton — wife of the absent Lt. Colonel Pemberton, presently stationed in Assam — has stated to Inspector Rao's inquiry that she spent the entirety of Thursday evening at the Bowring Institute, participating in a bridge tournament that she claims concluded at half past midnight. "My partner, Mr. Krishnaswamy, can confirm every hand," she reportedly told investigators with considerable composure.

The Bowring Institute, however, tells a markedly different story. The club secretary, Mr. Bernard Lobo, responded in writing to our enquiry this morning: "No bridge tournament was scheduled for the 16th of October. The Bowring Institute was closed to members from the afternoon of the 14th owing to emergency plumbing repairs in the card room. These repairs were not completed until the morning of the 17th. No member entered the building during this period." The letter is dated this very day and bears the official club seal.

"I have said all I intend to say without my solicitor present. The Inspector is welcome to verify anything he likes," Mrs. Pemberton told our correspondent when approached at her residence on Residency Road this morning, dressed, it must be noted, in full mourning black.

Neighbours on Richards Town Road report that Mrs. Pemberton and Major Henderson were long acquainted — she was, by several accounts, a frequent guest at 'The Silver Oaks' during Lt. Colonel Pemberton's extended deployments. Whether this acquaintance has any bearing on the events of Thursday night is not for this correspondent to speculate. It is, however, a matter this newspaper will continue to follow with considerable interest.

A source close to the inquiry informs us that Mrs. Pemberton's touring car — a pale green Morris Ten — was observed by a constable on Richards Town Road at 11:05 PM on the evening in question. The constable, who declined to be named, has since submitted a written statement to Inspector Rao.

— END OF MEMO —
